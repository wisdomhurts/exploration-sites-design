# External Benchmarks — What Award-Winning Looks Like

Curated references the review agent anchors its "award-winning" judgment to. These are not internal Exploration Sites work — they are external institutional peers and cartographic-art sources whose visual standard we aspire to match or exceed.

When the review flags that a map is "not yet award-winning," it should be able to point to which of these it falls short against — typographic restraint, hierarchy, palette discipline, data density, or finish.

Drop reference images or PDFs into `references/` inside this skill folder as you collect them. The agent reads this file for conceptual anchoring; reference files are for visual anchoring during future calibration.

---

## 1. National Geographic regional maps

**Why they're the benchmark:** Typographic restraint, masterful hierarchy, warm palettes that let geography breathe. Every label has a defined role. Nothing is decorative — every element earns its place.

**What to steal:**
- Country labels as watermark-level presence (extreme tracking, light weight, desaturated neutral)
- Hillshade treatment: restrained, background-level, never competing
- Clean minimal north arrows, never ornamental compass roses
- Palette discipline: warm neutrals with one decisive accent

**Where to find them:** [nationalgeographic.com/maps](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/maps) — any regional atlas map. Their century-long cartographic tradition is the gold standard for institutional trust.

---

## 2. Reuters / Financial Times graphics

**Why they're the benchmark:** Data-dense legibility. Designed for editorial context — must be understood in seconds by a financial reader. Every graphic carries title, source, date, methodology note, clear legend.

**What to steal:**
- The 3-second read — graphic conveys its headline before any reading happens
- Mandatory attribution / date / source blocks treated as first-class design elements
- Restrained palette: neutrals plus one or two brand accents
- Clear typographic hierarchy: title → subtitle → annotation → fine print

**Where to find them:** Reuters Graphics ([reuters.com/graphics](https://www.reuters.com/graphics)), FT Visual & Data Journalism on LinkedIn. Search "mining" or "commodities" for directly relevant examples.

---

## 3. SRK Consulting NI 43-101 technical report maps

**Why they're the benchmark:** This is the direct industry peer. SRK, AMC, and similar tier-one consultants produce NI 43-101 figures that set the compliance and quality floor for mining IR. Matching their maps = shipping. Exceeding them = differentiating.

**What to steal:**
- Mandatory cartographic element presentation (title block, scale, legend, projection, source, date)
- Professional symbology — no ornamentation, no clip-art, every symbol has a legend entry
- Neatlines, coordinate graticules, and tick marks treated as finished detail
- Austere, defensible palette choices

**Where to find them:** Publicly filed NI 43-101 reports on SEDAR+ ([sedarplus.ca](https://www.sedarplus.ca/)). Search any junior mining company and open the maps in their most recent technical report.

---

## 4. Agnico Eagle / Newmont corporate presentation maps

**Why they're the benchmark:** Senior gold miners with institutional design budgets. Their investor-day decks and annual reports carry maps that have been art-directed — not just GIS-exported. This is what a $50M market-cap junior should aspire to, and what a $5B+ producer treats as table stakes.

**What to steal:**
- Restrained confidence in palette — they never shout with color
- Portfolio-level maps that compress global footprint into a single readable figure
- Consistent figure-number / title block treatment across a whole deck
- Logo treated as subordinate, not a repeating motif

**Where to find them:** Latest Agnico Eagle and Newmont investor presentations on their IR pages. Look for "portfolio," "asset locations," or "project map" slides specifically.

---

## 5. Skeena Resources / Seabridge Gold investor decks

**Why they're the benchmark:** Institutional-grade **junior** peer. Proves that the art direction above is achievable at junior-company budget. Skeena's Eskay Creek maps and Seabridge's KSM / Iskut maps are the standard an Exploration Sites map should match for a comparable client.

**What to steal:**
- Strong project-level storytelling through a single map
- Claims boundaries and tenement outlines treated as finished linework, not raw GIS output
- Commodity color used surgically (one accent per map), not decoratively
- Callouts: restrained, no borders, text halos only

**Where to find them:** Skeena Resources and Seabridge Gold corporate presentations on their IR pages. Search for "Eskay Creek" (Skeena) and "KSM" or "Iskut" (Seabridge).

## Calibration notes

- These five are the **reference north star**, not replacements for the RUBRIC or PATTERNS files. The RUBRIC defines exact specs; BENCHMARKS defines taste.
- When a reviewer asks *"is this institutional enough?"* the answer is: would this map feel at home in any of the five sources above? If not, what specifically is missing — the hierarchy of NatGeo, the restraint of Reuters, the compliance of SRK, the confidence of Agnico, the surgical accent discipline of Skeena?
- As internal Exploration Sites work reaches or exceeds these benchmarks, rotate it into this file as an internal benchmark alongside the external ones. The ceiling should keep rising.
